## Our Content Audit Process

We follow a four-stage methodology: Inventory, Classify, Analyze, and Recommend. Each stage builds on the last to produce a comprehensive picture of your content's health and a clear action plan.

### Stage 1: Inventory

We catalog every page on your website — service pages, location pages, blog posts, landing pages, resource pages, and any other content accessible to search engines. For each page, we record:

- URL and page title
- Publication date and last modification date
- Word count
- Target keyword (if identifiable)
- Content type (service page, blog post, location page, informational)
- Indexation status (indexed, noindexed, excluded by Google)

The inventory provides the complete picture. Many businesses are surprised by what it reveals — duplicate pages from old site migrations that were never cleaned up, draft content that was accidentally published, landing pages from campaigns that ended years ago, and pages created by previous agencies that no longer serve any purpose.

### Stage 2: Classify

Every page is classified into one of five performance categories based on quantitative data from Google Analytics and Google Search Console:

**High Performers:** Pages generating significant organic traffic and/or conversions. These are your content assets — the pages worth investing in, updating, and building links to.

**Moderate Performers:** Pages generating some organic traffic but below their potential — often ranking on page 2 or in positions 7-10 on page 1. These are your biggest opportunities. A targeted optimization push can move them into high-performing territory.

**Underperformers:** Pages with minimal organic traffic despite being indexed and targeting legitimate keywords. These need diagnosis — is the content thin? Is the keyword too competitive? Is the page technically sound but lacking authority?

**Zero-Traffic Pages:** Pages generating zero organic visits over the previous 12 months. These are either targeting keywords with no search volume, are not indexed, or have been superseded by better-performing pages on the same topic.

**Harmful Pages:** Pages that are actively damaging your site's performance — cannibalizing higher-performing pages, containing outdated or inaccurate information that erodes E-E-A-T signals, or creating crawl waste that consumes budget without contributing value.

### Stage 3: Analyze

With pages classified, we conduct deep analysis on the factors driving each page's performance:

**Content Performance Metrics**

For every page, we pull:

- **Organic traffic:** Sessions from organic search over the past 12 months, with monthly trend data
- **Keyword rankings:** Which queries drive impressions and clicks, current positions, and position trends
- **Click-through rate (CTR):** Compared to expected CTR for the page's average position — low CTR relative to position indicates title tag or meta description issues
- **Engagement metrics:** Time on page, bounce rate, scroll depth (where available) — indicators of content quality and relevance
- **Conversions:** Phone calls, form submissions, and other conversion events attributed to each page
- **Backlinks:** External links pointing to each page, contributing to its authority

**Keyword Cannibalization Identification**

Keyword cannibalization occurs when multiple pages on your site target the same keyword, forcing Google to choose which one to rank — and often choosing neither. This is one of the most common and most damaging content issues we find.

We map every page's target keywords and identify overlaps:

- Two service pages targeting "AC repair" with different URL structures
- A blog post and a service page competing for "roof replacement cost"
- Multiple location pages targeting the same service keyword without geographic differentiation

Cannibalization is especially common on sites that have been adding content for years without a centralized keyword strategy. Resolving it — through consolidation, differentiation, or canonical signals — often produces immediate ranking improvements.

**Thin Content Identification**

Google defines thin content as pages that provide little or no added value. This includes:

- Pages with fewer than 300 words of substantive content
- Pages that are substantially duplicated from other pages on the same site or from external sources
- Auto-generated content with no editorial value
- Doorway pages (thin location pages with only the city name swapped)
- Pages with high ad-to-content ratio or interstitial-heavy designs

Thin content does not just fail to rank — it can drag down your entire domain's perceived quality in Google's eyes. Identifying and addressing thin content is one of the highest-impact actions a content audit can recommend.

**Content Gap Analysis**

The gap analysis examines what your competitors cover that you do not. We pull the keyword profiles of your top 5 ranking competitors and identify:

- **Topics they rank for that you have no content addressing.** A plumbing company ranking for "water heater installation" whose competitor also ranks for "tankless vs. tank water heater," "water heater repair vs. replacement," and "how long do water heaters last" has clear content gaps.
- **Content types they use that you do not.** FAQ sections, comparison pages, cost guides, how-to content, video content.
- **Content depth gaps.** Your competitor's service page has 2,000 words of detailed, structured content. Yours has 400 words. Google is showing its preference through rankings.
- **Emerging topics.** New search queries gaining volume in your industry that nobody in your market has addressed yet — first-mover opportunities.

Content gaps represent the clearest path to traffic growth. You are not taking traffic from competitors — you are capturing searches that you were previously invisible for.

**Content Freshness Assessment**

Google values fresh content, especially for topics where accuracy changes over time. We flag:

- Pages referencing outdated statistics, regulations, or best practices
- Blog posts with year references ("2023 Guide to...") that have not been updated
- Service pages describing processes or technologies that have changed
- Location pages with outdated information (old addresses, closed landmarks, changed area codes)

Stale content erodes trust with both users and search engines. Updating high-potential stale content is often the lowest-effort, highest-impact action a content audit recommends.

### Stage 4: Recommend

Every page receives one of five recommendations:

**Update:** The page has a valid target keyword, some existing authority (backlinks or historical rankings), and content that can be improved. We specify exactly what to update — expand thin sections, add current data, refresh examples, improve internal linking, optimize the title tag.

**Consolidate:** Multiple pages targeting similar keywords should be merged into a single, comprehensive page. The strongest URL is chosen as the target, content from weaker pages is integrated, and the weaker URLs are 301-redirected. This concentrates ranking signals instead of diluting them.

**Expand:** The page performs well but covers the topic incompletely. We identify specific subtopics, questions, and content sections that would strengthen the page's topical authority and improve rankings.

**Prune:** The page provides no value — zero traffic, no backlinks, thin content, no realistic keyword opportunity. These pages should be noindexed or removed entirely (with 301 redirects if they have any existing URLs referenced elsewhere). Pruning reduces crawl waste and improves the overall quality signal of your domain.

**Redirect:** The page's URL has value (backlinks, existing bookmarks) but the content is no longer relevant. 301 redirect to the most relevant active page to preserve link equity.

## Prioritization Framework

Not all recommendations are created equal. We score every recommended action on two dimensions:

**Expected impact (1-5):** How much will this action improve traffic, rankings, or conversions?

**Implementation effort (1-5):** How much time and resources does this action require?

Actions are prioritized by impact-to-effort ratio:

- **Quick wins (high impact, low effort):** Update title tags on underperforming pages. Add missing internal links. Refresh statistics in existing high-traffic posts. These are executed first.
- **Strategic projects (high impact, high effort):** Create comprehensive new content for gap topics. Consolidate cannibalized page groups. These enter the 60-90 day roadmap.
- **Maintenance items (moderate impact, low effort):** Update date references. Fix broken internal links. Add FAQ schema to existing FAQ sections.
- **Long-term investments (moderate impact, high effort):** Build entirely new content clusters. Develop new content formats (video, tools, interactive content).

This framework ensures you always know what to work on next and why — preventing the paralysis that comes from a 50-item to-do list with no prioritization.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does a content audit take?

For sites with up to 100 pages, the audit takes 5-7 business days. Sites with 100-500 pages take 7-10 business days. Enterprise sites with 500+ pages are scoped individually. The timeline includes data collection, analysis, recommendation development, and quality review. Larger sites require more time for the cannibalization and gap analyses, which scale with content volume.

### Will you recommend deleting content?

Pruning is sometimes the right recommendation — but we never recommend deleting content without data-backed justification. Pages with zero organic traffic, no backlinks, thin content, and no realistic keyword opportunity are candidates for removal. Pages with any external backlinks are redirected rather than deleted to preserve link equity. We always err on the side of updating or consolidating before pruning.

### Can I do a content audit myself?

You can pull basic traffic data from Google Analytics and Search Console. But a comprehensive content audit requires keyword mapping across your entire site (to identify cannibalization), competitive gap analysis (to identify missing topics), and strategic prioritization (to determine which actions will move the needle most). The analysis layer is where the value lives — raw data without interpretation is just a spreadsheet.

### How does a content audit differ from a technical SEO audit?

A [technical SEO audit](/seo-audit-services/technical-seo-audit/) examines your website's infrastructure — crawlability, speed, indexation, and schema. A content audit examines the content itself — what topics you cover, how well each page performs, where gaps exist, and which pages help or hurt your rankings. Both are components of our comprehensive audit process, and both inform different aspects of your SEO strategy. Technical issues prevent Google from accessing your content. Content issues prevent Google from valuing it.

### How often should I audit my content?

We recommend a comprehensive content audit annually, with quarterly performance reviews of your top pages and any new content published. The competitive landscape evolves — new content from competitors, emerging search queries, and algorithm updates all change the relative performance of your content. Annual re-audits keep your content strategy aligned with current opportunities.
