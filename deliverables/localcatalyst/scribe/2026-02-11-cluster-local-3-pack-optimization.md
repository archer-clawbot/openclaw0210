## Google Local 3-Pack: How to Win the Most Valuable Real Estate in Local Search

The Google local 3-pack is the three-business map listing that appears at the top of search results whenever Google detects local intent. For local businesses, earning a position in this 3-pack is the highest-impact outcome that [local SEO services](/services/) can deliver — and it is the objective around which our entire strategy is built.

Research consistently shows that the local 3-pack captures approximately 42% of all clicks on local search results pages. That number is staggering. It means that nearly half of all people who search for a local service click on one of the three map results before looking at anything else — before scrolling to organic results, before considering paid ads, and before engaging with AI Overviews.

If your business occupies one of those three positions, you are getting the lion's share of search-driven customer inquiries. If you do not, you are fighting over the remaining 58% with every other business in your market. ## What Exactly Is the Local 3-Pack?

The local 3-pack (also called the Map Pack or local pack) is a SERP feature that displays three Google Business Profile listings alongside a map snippet. It appears for queries with local intent — searches where Google determines the user is looking for a nearby business or service.

Each listing in the 3-pack displays:

- Business name
- Star rating and review count
- Business category
- Address (or service area indicator)
- Hours (open/closed status)
- Phone number
- Website link
- Driving directions link

The 3-pack appears above organic results, which means it receives the first visual attention from searchers. For mobile users — who now account for the majority of local searches — the 3-pack often dominates the entire above-the-fold screen.

Below the three listings, a "More businesses" or "More places" link expands to the full Google Maps interface, where positions 4 through 20+ are visible. But the click-through rate drops dramatically after position 3. The difference between ranking #3 and #4 in the local pack is not incremental — it is the difference between being visible on the search results page and being hidden behind an additional click.

## Ranking Factors Specific to the 3-Pack

The local 3-pack draws from Google Maps results, so the same three core ranking factors apply: relevance, distance, and prominence. However, the competitive dynamics of the 3-pack introduce additional nuances. For a detailed breakdown of these core factors, see our [Google Maps ranking guide](/services/google-maps-ranking/).

### GBP Completeness and Category Optimization

Google's local ranking algorithm weighs GBP signals more heavily than any other factor category for 3-pack rankings. Studies by BrightLocal, Whitespark, and other local SEO research firms consistently place GBP signals at 32-36% of total ranking factor weight.

The specific GBP elements that influence 3-pack positioning:

- **Primary category** — This is the single most powerful ranking signal in local SEO. Your primary category must exactly match the core service you want to rank for. A business with "Dentist" as its primary category will outrank one with "Dental Clinic" for searches containing the word "dentist," all else being equal.
- **Additional categories** — Secondary categories expand the queries for which you are eligible to appear. Most businesses should use 3-7 additional categories that reflect genuine service offerings.
- **Profile completeness** — Google tracks a completeness metric internally. Profiles with every field populated — description, services, products, attributes, hours, photos, Q&A — receive a ranking advantage over incomplete profiles. Our [GBP optimization service](/services/gbp-optimization/) ensures no field is left empty.

### Review Signals

Reviews function as both a prominence signal and a trust signal for 3-pack ranking. The algorithm evaluates:

- **Review count** — More reviews signal higher prominence. The threshold for competitiveness varies by market. In some industries, 50 reviews are sufficient; in others, 300+ is the baseline.
- **Average rating** — Businesses with 4.0+ ratings consistently outperform lower-rated competitors. However, a perfect 5.0 with few reviews carries less weight than a 4.6 with hundreds.
- **Review velocity** — A steady flow of new reviews signals ongoing business activity and customer satisfaction. A business that received 100 reviews two years ago and none since sends a weaker signal than one that receives 5-10 reviews per month consistently.
- **Review content** — Reviews that mention specific services, locations, or keywords contribute to relevance signals. A review stating "They did an excellent job on our kitchen remodel" reinforces relevance for "kitchen remodel" queries.
- **Owner responses** — Responding to reviews (both positive and negative) demonstrates engagement. Google has indicated that responsiveness to reviews is a ranking consideration.

### Proximity and the 3-Pack

Distance is the ranking factor that creates the most frustration for local businesses, because it is largely outside your control. The 3-pack displayed to a searcher standing outside your front door will be different from the 3-pack displayed to someone three miles away.

Key proximity dynamics in the 3-pack:

- **The centroid bias** — Businesses located closer to a city or neighborhood center often have a geographic advantage because more searchers are located nearby. A business at the edge of town has fewer "close" searchers by definition.
- **Search radius varies by industry** — Google adjusts the geographic scope of results based on business type. For "coffee shop near me," the radius might be 1-2 miles. For "personal injury attorney near me," it might be 10-20 miles.
- **Proximity can be partially offset by prominence** — A highly prominent business (strong reviews, strong citations, strong links) can outrank a closer but less prominent competitor. This offset has limits, but it is real and measurable.

### Behavioral Signals

Google tracks how users interact with 3-pack listings and uses that data as a ranking signal:

- **Click-through rate** — Listings that receive more clicks for a given query send a positive signal.
- **Driving direction requests** — High direction request volume signals real-world relevance.
- **Call clicks** — Phone calls initiated from the listing indicate business engagement.
- **Website visits** — Clicks to your website from your GBP listing are tracked and factored.
- **Engagement depth** — How long users interact with your expanded GBP (viewing photos, reading reviews, exploring services) may influence future ranking.

## Local Pack Filtering and Diversity

Google applies diversity filters to the 3-pack to prevent a single business type or chain from dominating the results. Understanding these filters helps explain some otherwise confusing ranking behavior:

- **Brand diversity** — Google rarely shows multiple locations of the same brand in the 3-pack. If you have three locations, typically only the one closest to the searcher will appear.
- **Category diversity** — For broad queries, Google may diversify the 3-pack across sub-categories. A search for "auto repair" might show a general mechanic, a transmission specialist, and a body shop.
- **Justification snippets** — Google sometimes adds blue justification text below a listing explaining why it was included (e.g., "Mentioned in reviews: transmission repair"). These justifications indicate that Google is matching your listing to the query through review or post content, not just categories.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many businesses can appear in the local 3-pack?

By default, Google displays three businesses in the local pack — hence the name "3-pack." However, Google occasionally expands this to four listings through an ad placement (a sponsored listing at the top of the pack) or through various testing layouts. The organic (non-paid) positions remain three. In the expanded Maps view, Google displays up to 20 results, but only the top three appear on the main search results page.

### Is the local 3-pack the same as Google Maps results?

The 3-pack is a condensed view of Google Maps results displayed on the Google search results page. The top three Google Maps results for a given query and location are pulled into the 3-pack. Clicking "More businesses" opens the full Google Maps interface. So the 3-pack is essentially a preview of the Maps results — ranking in the 3-pack means ranking in the top 3 of Google Maps for that specific query and searcher location.

### Can paid ads get my business into the 3-pack?

Google offers Local Services Ads (LSAs) and Google Ads with location extensions that can place your business at the top of local results. However, these are labeled as "Sponsored" and appear above or alongside the organic 3-pack, not within it. The organic 3-pack positions cannot be purchased — they are earned through optimization. Most consumers recognize the difference, and organic 3-pack listings carry higher trust. Our approach focuses on earning organic 3-pack positions through sustained optimization, though we can advise on paid strategies for immediate visibility while organic rankings build.

### Why do I see different 3-pack results than my colleague across town?

The local 3-pack is personalized based on the searcher's exact location. Google calculates distance from each searcher to each eligible business, and proximity is a significant ranking factor. Your colleague across town is physically closer to different businesses than you are, so their 3-pack will reflect those different proximity calculations. This is exactly why geo-grid tracking is essential — it shows you how your rankings vary across your entire service area rather than just from a single location.
