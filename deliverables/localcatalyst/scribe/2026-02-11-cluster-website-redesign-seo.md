## Why Website Redesigns Kill Rankings

A website redesign is one of the highest-risk events in a business's SEO lifecycle. Here is why rankings tank when redesigns are handled by teams that do not understand search:

### URL Changes Without Redirects

Every URL on your site has accumulated authority over time — from backlinks, internal links, user engagement signals, and indexation history. When a URL changes and there is no 301 redirect telling Google "this old URL now lives at this new URL," all of that accumulated authority evaporates. The new URL starts from zero. Google treats it as a brand new page with no history.

If you had 200 backlinks pointing to `/services/plumbing-repair/` and the redesign changes it to `/our-services/plumbing/` without a redirect, those 200 backlinks now point to a 404 page. The authority they carried is lost.

### Content That Disappears

Design agencies frequently remove pages during a redesign — "streamlining" the navigation, consolidating services, or simply deciding certain pages do not fit the new design. Every deleted page that was generating organic traffic represents lost rankings and lost revenue.

The pages most commonly deleted are also often the most SEO-valuable: long-form blog posts, location pages, older service pages, and resource content that had accumulated backlinks and rankings over years.

### Platform Changes

Migrating from one CMS to another (Wix to WordPress, Squarespace to WordPress, WordPress to Webflow, or any other combination) introduces a cascade of technical changes that affect SEO:

- URL structures change (Wix uses `/post/` prefixes, Squarespace uses date-based URLs, WordPress uses custom permalinks)
- Internal linking architectures change
- Schema markup implementations break
- Site speed profiles change dramatically
- Canonical tags, meta robots directives, and XML sitemaps may all require reconfiguration
- JavaScript rendering behavior changes

Each of these is a potential ranking disruption, and platform migrations compound them all simultaneously.

### Internal Linking Architecture Changes

Your internal links are a critical ranking signal. They tell Google how your content is organized, which pages are most important, and how topics relate to each other. A redesign that restructures navigation, changes menu hierarchy, or reorganizes content categories fundamentally alters these signals — often without anyone realizing it.

## Our SEO-Safe Redesign Process

We have developed a six-phase redesign process designed to preserve 100% of your existing SEO equity while delivering the design improvements your business needs. This process applies whether we are designing from scratch or partnering with your design team to ensure nothing breaks.

### Phase 1: Pre-Launch SEO Audit

Before any design work begins, we establish a complete baseline of your current SEO performance:

- **Full site crawl** — Every URL, its status code, internal links, and technical attributes
- **Ranking baseline** — Current keyword rankings for every term driving traffic (we use our geo-grid and SoLV methodology to capture local ranking positions with precision)
- **Traffic baseline** — Page-level organic traffic for the previous 12 months
- **Backlink inventory** — Every external link pointing to your site, mapped to the specific URL it targets
- **Content inventory** — Every page on your site, its traffic value, ranking keywords, and backlink count
- **Technical baseline** — Current Core Web Vitals scores, schema markup, robots.txt, XML sitemap, canonical configuration

This audit becomes the reference document for the entire redesign. Nothing moves forward without it. It also integrates with the [SEO audit](/seo-audit-services/) process that underpins all of our client engagements.

### Phase 2: URL Mapping and Redirect Plan

Every URL on the current site is mapped to its corresponding URL on the new site:

| Old URL | New URL | Redirect Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `/services/plumbing-repair/` | `/plumbing/repair-services/` | 301 | 47 backlinks, 320 monthly visits |
| `/blog/water-heater-guide/` | `/resources/water-heater-guide/` | 301 | 12 backlinks, 890 monthly visits |
| `/about-us/` | `/about/` | 301 | 3 backlinks |
| `/scottsdale-plumber/` | `/locations/scottsdale/` | 301 | 28 backlinks, 510 monthly visits |

Every row in this map is verified, tested, and documented before launch. No URL changes without a corresponding redirect.

When possible, we recommend preserving existing URL structures entirely. If `/services/plumbing-repair/` is ranking well and has backlinks, the ideal outcome is keeping that exact URL on the new site. A redirect that is not needed is a redirect that cannot break.

### Phase 3: Content Migration Plan

Every page on the current site is categorized:

- **Migrate as-is** — Pages with strong traffic, rankings, and backlinks that should transfer to the new site with their content preserved
- **Migrate and improve** — Pages with ranking potential that would benefit from content updates during the redesign
- **Consolidate** — Multiple thin pages that should be merged into a single, stronger page (with redirects from the deprecated URLs)
- **Retire** — Pages with zero traffic, zero backlinks, and no strategic value (still requires a redirect to the nearest relevant page)

No page is deleted without documentation of its traffic history, backlink profile, and ranking keywords. The decision to remove any page requires sign-off based on data, not design preference.

### Phase 4: Design and Development (With SEO Guardrails)

During the build phase, we provide ongoing SEO oversight:

- **Template-level review** — Every page template is reviewed for heading structure, schema markup integration, internal linking capability, and Core Web Vitals compliance
- **Mobile-first validation** — Every template is tested for mobile usability, tap targets, viewport configuration, and responsive behavior
- **Speed engineering** — Image optimization, lazy loading, critical CSS, font optimization, and JavaScript management are built into the development process, not bolted on afterward
- **Schema implementation** — All structured data (LocalBusiness, Service, BreadcrumbList, FAQ, Review) is implemented during development, not after launch
- **Navigation architecture** — Menu structure, footer links, and sidebar navigation are designed to support both user experience and internal link distribution

This is the same technical discipline we apply to every [WordPress SEO](/seo-website-design/wordpress-seo/) build and [landing page design](/seo-website-design/landing-page-design/) engagement.

### Phase 5: Pre-Launch Testing

Before the new site goes live, we conduct a comprehensive pre-launch checklist:

- **Redirect testing** — Every redirect in the map is tested to confirm it resolves correctly (not redirect chains, not loops, not 404s)
- **Crawl simulation** — Full crawl of the staging site to identify broken links, orphan pages, crawl errors, and technical issues
- **Schema validation** — All structured data tested via Google's Rich Results Test
- **Core Web Vitals** — Speed testing on the staging environment to confirm performance targets are met
- **Content verification** — Spot-check critical pages to confirm content migrated correctly (title tags, meta descriptions, heading structures, images, internal links)
- **Canonical and indexation directives** — Confirm that no staging site noindex tags will carry over to production
- **XML sitemap** — Generated, validated, and ready for submission to Google Search Console

### Phase 6: Post-Launch Monitoring

The 90 days after launch are the most critical period. We monitor daily:

- **Crawl behavior** — Google Search Console crawl stats to confirm Googlebot is discovering and crawling the new URLs
- **Indexation** — URL Inspection tool to verify new pages are being indexed and old URLs are being processed through redirects
- **Ranking tracking** — Daily keyword monitoring with immediate alerts for any ranking drops exceeding 3 positions
- **Traffic comparison** — Page-level traffic compared to pre-redesign baseline
- **Redirect monitoring** — Ongoing verification that all redirects remain functional
- **Error monitoring** — 404 errors in Search Console (crawled but not found) that indicate missed redirects

If rankings dip — which can happen temporarily even with a perfect migration — we have the diagnostic tools to identify the cause and respond within hours, not weeks.

## Handling Platform Changes

Platform migrations add complexity to every phase of the redesign. Here is what to expect for the most common migration paths:

### Wix to WordPress

Wix URLs follow non-standard patterns (`/post/`, `/blog-1/`, custom subdomains) that require careful mapping to WordPress permalink structures. Wix does not provide native redirect management for migrated domains, so redirects must be implemented at the server or hosting level immediately upon migration.

### Squarespace to WordPress

Squarespace uses date-based blog URLs and collection-based page URLs. The built-in URL redirect feature has limitations on pattern matching and does not support regex. Server-level redirects (via .htaccess or hosting provider) handle the migration more reliably.

### WordPress to WordPress (Theme or Builder Change)

Changing themes or page builders within WordPress can still break URL structures, internal linking, and schema markup if the new theme handles slugs, categories, or custom post types differently. This is the most common "low-risk" migration that catches businesses off guard.

### Any Platform to Headless/Jamstack

Migrations to headless CMS platforms (Next.js, Gatsby, Nuxt) introduce JavaScript rendering challenges. Google can render JavaScript, but the process is slower and less reliable than server-rendered HTML. Pre-rendering, server-side rendering (SSR), and careful Googlebot testing are mandatory.

## Common Redesign Mistakes That Kill SEO

| Mistake | Impact | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **No redirect plan** | Complete authority loss on changed URLs | Phase 2: URL mapping before design starts |
| **Dropping pages that rank** | Immediate traffic and ranking loss | Phase 3: Data-driven content migration decisions |
| **Redirect chains** (old → intermediate → new) | Authority dilution, crawl budget waste | Direct 301s from old URL to final URL |
| **Staging site noindex tags in production** | Entire site deindexed | Phase 5: Pre-launch directive verification |
| **Losing backlinks** | Authority signals disappear | Phase 1: Backlink inventory with URL-level mapping |

We provide clear documentation and checkpoint reviews throughout the process. This collaborative model ensures your design team can focus on what they do best while we protect your search equity.

### What if our rankings have already dropped after a redesign?

We offer post-redesign recovery services. The process starts with a [comprehensive SEO audit](/seo-audit-services/) comparing the current site to whatever baseline data is available from before the redesign. We identify missing redirects, lost content, broken technical elements, and any other ranking factors that changed. Recovery typically takes 2-5 months depending on the severity of the damage.

### Is it ever safe to change URL structures during a redesign?

Yes — with proper redirect implementation. There are valid reasons to change URLs: improving readability, creating logical hierarchies, or aligning with a new keyword strategy. The key is that every old URL has a 301 redirect to its new location, and no URL changes happen without data-informed justification. Changing URLs "because the new structure looks cleaner" is not sufficient justification if the old structure was ranking.
