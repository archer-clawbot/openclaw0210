# Sandbag Distribution Centers: Where to Get Emergency Sandbags Near You

When flooding threatens, knowing where to get sandbags fast can mean the difference between a protected home and a flooded one. Most counties and municipalities operate sandbag distribution centers during declared flood emergencies — and many keep permanent programs running year-round for residents in high-risk areas.

This guide explains how public distribution programs work, what to expect when you arrive, and how to find your local center quickly when every minute counts.

## How Public Sandbag Distribution Works

Emergency sandbag distribution is typically organized at the county or city level and activated when flood conditions are forecast. Here's what the process generally looks like:

**Activation:** When the National Weather Service issues a Flood Watch or Warning, local emergency management offices coordinate with public works and fire departments to open distribution sites.

**Locations:** Distribution sites are typically set up at:
- Fire stations and fire department training grounds
- Public works yards and maintenance facilities
- Community centers and fairgrounds
- Parks department sites with large paved areas

**What's typically provided:**
- Empty sandbags (most common) — you fill them yourself on-site or at home
- Pre-filled sandbags at some locations — usually limited quantities
- Access to sand piles for filling
- Shovels (sometimes) — bring your own to be safe

**Typical limits:** Many programs cap distribution at 10–25 bags per household for equitable access during high demand. Commercial properties and large-scale requests are typically referred to private suppliers.

**What to bring:**
- Valid ID (some programs require county residency verification)
- Your own shovels and gloves
- A truck, SUV, or trailer — passenger cars can be loaded, but a 50-bag load (roughly 1,500 lbs filled) may exceed capacity
- Water and snacks — lines can be long during major events

## Finding Your Local Sandbag Distribution Center

The fastest way to find your local distribution center is through official channels:

1. **Call 211** — The United Way's 211 helpline connects to local emergency resources and is often updated in real time during disasters.
2. **Your county emergency management website** — Search "[your county] emergency management sandbags"
3. **Local fire department non-emergency line**
4. **City or county public works department**
5. **Local news outlets** — TV stations and newspapers post distribution site lists rapidly during flood events

## State-by-State Distribution Program Overview

Programs vary by state and locality. Below is an overview of how major states typically structure their programs, along with key contacts for finding local distribution.

### Alabama

Alabama's county emergency management agencies (EMAs) coordinate sandbag distribution through the Alabama Emergency Management Agency (AEMA). During declared emergencies, AEMA can mobilize state sandbag reserves to local distribution points. Many counties in the Mobile Bay and Tennessee Valley regions maintain standing inventories. **Contact:** aema.alabama.gov

### Arkansas

The Arkansas Division of Emergency Management (ADEM) activates county-level distribution during River District and delta region flood events. Craighead, Mississippi, and Phillips counties have historically maintained year-round sandbag programs due to persistent flood risk. **Contact:** adem.arkansas.gov

### California

California's county Office of Emergency Services offices activate sandbag programs for winter storm and atmospheric river events. Southern California programs (Los Angeles, San Bernardino) focus on post-wildfire flood risk. The Sacramento region activates annually during high Sierra snowmelt season. Many California cities maintain free year-round fill-it-yourself programs at local fire stations. **Contact:** caloes.ca.gov

### Colorado

Colorado's Floodplain Management programs are overseen by the Division of Homeland Security and Emergency Management. The South Platte and Arkansas River corridors see the most distribution program activations. El Paso, Pueblo, and Larimer counties have robust programs. **Contact:** dhsem.state.co.us

### Florida

Florida's Division of Emergency Management coordinates with county emergency managers for hurricane and tropical storm flood response. South Florida programs (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach) are heavily organized and can process thousands of residents per day at major distribution sites. FEMA pre-positions sandbag resources in Florida before hurricane season. **Contact:** floridadisaster.org

### Georgia

The Georgia Emergency Management and Homeland Security Agency (GEMA/HS) coordinates state sandbag reserves available to counties during declared emergencies. Coastal counties (Chatham, Camden, Glynn) and Savannah River communities have the most active programs. **Contact:** gema.georgia.gov

### Illinois

Illinois Emergency Management Agency (IEMA) manages state sandbag reserves deployed through county EMA offices. The Illinois and Mississippi River corridors — including Madison, St. Clair, and Adams counties — have permanent sandbag programs due to chronic flood risk. **Contact:** iema.illinois.gov

### Indiana

Indiana Department of Homeland Security (IDHS) coordinates with county emergency managers for sandbag distribution during Ohio River and Wabash River flood events. Posey, Vanderburgh, and Knox counties have historically robust programs. **Contact:** in.gov/dhs

### Iowa

Iowa Homeland Security and Emergency Management (HSEMD) maintains one of the country's most active sandbag programs due to the Mississippi, Missouri, Cedar, and Des Moines River systems. During major events, the state can deploy millions of sandbags through county distribution networks. **Contact:** homelandsecurity.iowa.gov

### Kansas

Kansas Division of Emergency Management activates county programs along the Kansas, Arkansas, and Neosho River corridors. Wyandotte, Douglas, and Butler counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** kdem.ks.gov

### Kentucky

Kentucky Emergency Management (KYEM) coordinates with county programs along the Ohio, Licking, Big Sandy, and Cumberland River systems. Eastern Kentucky and Louisville metro programs are most frequently activated. **Contact:** ready.ky.gov

### Louisiana

Louisiana Homeland Security and Emergency Management (GOHSEP) operates one of the country's most experienced and organized sandbag distribution networks. Southeast Louisiana parishes — Jefferson, St. Tammany, Lafourche, Terrebonne, and others — maintain year-round programs and can rapidly scale during hurricane season. See our [Louisiana sandbag guide](/sandbags-louisiana/) for detailed parish-level information. **Contact:** gohsep.la.gov

### Michigan

Michigan State Police Emergency Management activates county programs primarily for western Michigan and the Detroit metro region during spring thaw and heavy rain events. **Contact:** michigan.gov/msp/divisions/emhsd

### Minnesota

Minnesota Division of Homeland Security and Emergency Management (HSEM) activates the state sandbag reserve system through county EMAs during Red River and Minnesota River flood events. The Red River Valley (Clay, Norman, Polk counties) has some of the nation's most organized sandbag programs due to near-annual flooding. **Contact:** hsem.dps.mn.gov

### Mississippi

Mississippi Emergency Management Agency (MEMA) coordinates state and county programs along the Mississippi River delta and Gulf Coast. Washington, Bolivar, and Harrison counties have the most established programs. **Contact:** msema.org

### Missouri

Missouri State Emergency Management Agency (SEMA) maintains large sandbag reserves for Missouri River and Mississippi River corridor programs. St. Charles, Jefferson, and Cape Girardeau counties have historically high-volume distribution operations. **Contact:** sema.dps.mo.gov

### Nebraska

Nebraska Emergency Management Agency (NEMA) coordinates county programs along the Missouri, Platte, and Elkhorn River corridors. Douglas and Sarpy counties (Omaha metro) have robust programs; western counties activate for Platte River events. **Contact:** nema.nebraska.gov

### New Jersey

New Jersey Office of Emergency Management activates county programs along the Raritan, Passaic, and Delaware River corridors and for coastal storm surge events. Middlesex, Morris, Passaic, and Bergen counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** ready.nj.gov

### North Carolina

North Carolina Emergency Management activates county programs for hurricane, tropical storm, and mountain flooding events. Coastal plain counties (Beaufort, Bertie, Martin) and mountain counties for flash flood events. **Contact:** ncem.org

### North Dakota

North Dakota Department of Emergency Services activates county programs primarily for the Red River Valley — one of the most flood-prone regions in North America. Cass County (Fargo) has exceptionally well-organized distribution capability built through repeated major flood events. **Contact:** des.nd.gov

### Ohio

Ohio Emergency Management Agency coordinates with county EMAs for Ohio River and tributary flood distribution programs. Hamilton, Clermont, and Scioto counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** ema.ohio.gov

### Oklahoma

Oklahoma Emergency Management activates programs along the Arkansas, Canadian, and Red River corridors. Tulsa and Muskogee counties have the most robust programs. **Contact:** oklahoma.gov/oem

### South Carolina

South Carolina Emergency Management Division coordinates county programs for coastal flooding (Georgetown, Horry, Berkeley counties) and inland flooding along the Pee Dee, Congaree, and Savannah River systems. **Contact:** scemd.org

### Tennessee

Tennessee Emergency Management Agency (TEMA) coordinates county programs along the Tennessee, Cumberland, and Mississippi River systems. Shelby, Montgomery, and Humphreys counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** tn.gov/tema

### Texas

Texas Division of Emergency Management (TDEM) coordinates county programs for Gulf Coast hurricane flooding, Hill Country flash floods, and Red River events. Harris (Houston), Jefferson, and Orange counties have extensive distribution infrastructure. See our [Texas sandbag guide](/sandbags-texas/) for county-level information. **Contact:** tdem.texas.gov

### Virginia

Virginia Department of Emergency Management (VDEM) activates programs for coastal flooding (Hampton Roads), Roanoke, and James River valley events. Norfolk, Virginia Beach, and Chesterfield counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** vaemergency.gov

### Washington

Washington Emergency Management Division activates programs for Puget Sound coastal flooding and Snoqualmie, Puyallup, and Chehalis River events. King and Pierce counties have robust programs. **Contact:** mil.wa.gov/emergency-management-division

### West Virginia

West Virginia Division of Homeland Security and Emergency Management coordinates county programs along the Elk, Greenbrier, and Kanawha River corridors. Kanawha, Nicholas, and Fayette counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** dhsem.wv.gov

### Wisconsin

Wisconsin Emergency Management activates county programs along the Wisconsin, Mississippi, Fox, and Rock River systems. La Crosse and Grant counties have the most active programs. **Contact:** dma.wi.gov/wem

## If Distribution Centers Are Out of Stock: Private Sources

During large-scale flood events, public distribution centers often exhaust supplies within hours. Have these backup options ready:

1. **Big-box home improvement stores** — The Home Depot, Lowe's, and Menards often stock both empty bags and bagged sand
2. **Landscape supply companies** — Carry bulk sand and bags; prices are typically reasonable
3. **Online bulk suppliers** — For advance planning only; not useful for immediate needs
4. **[Sandbag delivery services](/sandbag-delivery-services/)** — Some suppliers offer emergency delivery in high-risk areas

See our [commercial sandbag uses guide](/commercial-sandbag-uses/) for information on bulk purchasing for business applications.

## Prepare Before You Need to Go

The best time to locate your distribution center is now — not during a flood warning when roads may be flooded and centers may be overwhelmed. Know your nearest location, its operating hours, and its capacity limits before flood season begins.
