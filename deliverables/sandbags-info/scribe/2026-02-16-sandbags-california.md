## H1: Sandbags in California: County Programs, Atmospheric Rivers, and Wildfire Debris Flows

California's flood risk has a complexity that most Americans outside the state don't fully appreciate. While the state's reputation for drought is accurate, California also ranks among the most flood-prone states in the country — and its flood hazards are changing as climate patterns intensify.

Two distinct flood threats drive sandbag demand in California:

1. **Atmospheric rivers** — concentrated bands of moisture that make landfall along the Pacific Coast and deliver months of rainfall in days
2. **Post-wildfire debris flows** — floods and mudslides in areas where recent wildfires have stripped hillsides of vegetation, dramatically increasing runoff and sediment load

Understanding which risk applies to your location shapes how you prepare — and what county resources are available.

---

## H2: California's Dual Flood Threat

### H3: Atmospheric Rivers

Atmospheric rivers are long, narrow corridors of concentrated water vapor in the atmosphere. A major atmospheric river event can carry more water than the Mississippi River — delivered to a relatively small geographic area over a 24–48 hour period.

The 2022–2023 California atmospheric river season caused over $11 billion in flood damage, breached levees in the Sacramento Valley, and flooded communities that hadn't seen serious flooding in decades. The 2024–2025 season brought the devastating Pineapple Express storms that triggered flooding across LA County.

Atmospheric river flooding primarily affects:
- Sacramento Valley and Delta communities
- The San Joaquin Valley
- Bay Area lowlands and riparian communities
- Coastal communities from San Diego to Humboldt County

### H3: Post-Wildfire Debris Flows

This is the hazard most specific to California. After large wildfires, the charred hillside acts like a parking lot — water that would previously have been absorbed by vegetation and soil instead runs directly off the slope, carrying debris, ash, boulders, and sediment at high velocity.

The 2018 Montecito debris flow killed 23 people in a matter of minutes following the Thomas Fire. The 2025 January storms produced debris flows in LA areas burned by the Palisades and Eaton fires.

Debris flows move differently than riverine flooding: they're fast, dense, and carry far more destructive force per unit volume than clean water. Sandbags that work for rising-water flooding offer limited protection against a debris flow, but they can redirect shallow debris-laden flows away from entry points.

## H2: Los Angeles County Sandbag Program

Los Angeles County operates one of the largest flood response programs in the country through its Department of Public Works.

### H3: How to Access Sandbags in LA County

**LA County Public Works Sandbag Program:**
- **Main line:** 800-675-4357 (800-675-HELP) — announces active distribution sites
- **Activated events:** During storm events, LA County DPW activates sandbag distribution at district yard locations throughout the county
- **Self-fill stations:** LA County has operated automated or self-service sand stations at some locations; call 800-675-4357 for current availability
- **Limits:** Typically 15–20 bags per household per visit

**City of Los Angeles (separate program):**
- The City of LA operates independently of the county program
- LA Bureau of Sanitation and LA Emergency Management: 213-473-3231
- During major storm events, LA City activates sites at fire stations and parks; check ReadyLA.org or call 311

### H3: Post-Fire Debris Flow Context in LA

Residents in communities directly downhill of recent wildfire burn scars need to understand that sandbags serve a different role than in standard riverine flooding. For debris flows:

- Sandbags can redirect flow but generally cannot stop a significant debris event
- The primary use is protecting door openings from shallow debris-laden water
- Berms redirecting flow away from structures are more important than door-level barriers
- Contact LA County Public Works for guidance specific to your burn scar location — they provide technical assistance for communities in the post-fire recovery period

## H2: San Diego County Sandbag Program

San Diego County faces both coastal flooding from major storm events and inland flash flooding, plus post-fire debris flow risk from the county's periodic wildfire seasons.

### H3: Accessing Sandbags in San Diego County

**San Diego County Office of Emergency Services:** 858-715-2000  
**San Diego County 211:** Activated during flood events with real-time distribution information

- County distribution sites typically activate at DPW facilities in Kearny Mesa and East County
- The City of San Diego runs its own program independently: call 619-236-5500 (Public Utilities) or 619-533-4636 (Storm Water)

**City of San Diego specifics:**
- San Diego's storm water division distributes sandbags year-round at the Kearny Mesa Operations Facility
- **Limits:** Typically 10 bags per household
- Pickup requires proof of San Diego residency during high-demand periods

### H3: San Diego's Wildfire-Flood Nexus

San Diego County has experienced multiple major wildfires in the 21st century (Cedar Fire 2003, Witch Fire 2007, Valley Fire 2020), each creating multi-year post-fire debris flow risk in affected watersheds. Residents near burn areas should register with San Diego County's emergency notification system (AlertSanDiego) and consult with the county's fire-focused flood preparation resources.

## H2: Sacramento and the Sacramento Valley

Sacramento sits at the confluence of the American River and Sacramento River — at the center of what the Association of State Floodplain Managers has called one of the most flood-threatened metropolitan areas in the United States.

### H3: Sacramento County Sandbag Distribution

**Sacramento County Public Works:** 916-875-4311  
**Sacramento City Utilities/Community Development:** 916-808-1000

- Sacramento maintains year-round sandbag distribution through its Public Works facilities — one of the few major California counties with non-emergency-only distribution
- During atmospheric river events, additional sites activate rapidly; check SacCounty.gov or call 916-875-4311
- **Limits:** Typically 10–15 bags per household at county sites

### H3: The Sacramento Valley Flood Context

The Sacramento Valley is protected by a complex levee system. Many levees in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta were built in the 19th century and have not been significantly updated. USACE (Army Corps of Engineers) and the State DWR (Department of Water Resources) have both identified significant levee failure risk in major flood events.

For individual homeowners in the Sacramento area:
- Understand your FEMA flood zone — many Sacramento neighborhoods are in mapped flood hazard areas
- The 100-year flood scenario for Sacramento involves water that can overtop sandbag protection — your evacuation plan matters as much as your barrier
- Sandbags are most valuable for the incremental events that happen between major system failures

## H2: Fresno and the San Joaquin Valley

Fresno and the broader San Joaquin Valley face a different flood profile: primarily agricultural and periurban flooding from irrigation district overflows, Kings River flooding, and intense fog-season rainfall events.

### H3: Fresno County Sandbag Access

**City of Fresno Public Works:** 559-621-5600  
**Fresno County Public Works:** 559-600-4259

- Fresno typically activates distribution through Public Works Service Centers during declared flood events
- The San Joaquin Valley's primary flood threat often involves longer-duration events (days of moderate rain) rather than the sudden intense events of Southern California
- **Kings River flooding** affects communities in eastern Fresno County; these areas should maintain personal stockpiles given limited municipal access

## H2: Statewide California Resources

**California Governor's Office of Emergency Services (Cal OES):** CalOES.ca.gov — coordinates statewide disaster response; provides county resource information during state of emergency declarations.

**California 211:** Statewide resource hotline activated during disaster events for real-time distribution information.

**Department of Water Resources Flood Information:** Water.ca.gov — DWR maintains flood forecast and levee system data relevant to Sacramento Valley residents.

**National Weather Service — Los Angeles:** weather.gov/lox — provides specific post-fire debris flow watches and warnings for Southern California

**FEMA Flood Map Service Center:** MSC.FEMA.gov — check your property's flood zone designation before flood season.

## H2: California Sandbag Preparation Tips

**For atmospheric river events (Northern California emphasis):**
- Monitor National Weather Service forecasts 5–7 days out — atmospheric rivers are trackable at that range
- Preparation window is typically 48–72 hours before landfall
- Focus on drainage as much as barriers — clear gutters, downspouts, and drains before the first rain band arrives
- See our [sandbag levee guide](/sandbag-levee/) for perimeter protection technique

**For post-fire debris flow risk (Southern California emphasis):**
- Consult with your county's post-fire recovery coordinator for site-specific guidance
- Focus on redirecting flow away from entry points with berm-style deployments
- Self-activating flood bags are useful for rapid doorway protection if you have limited setup time
- The primary protection strategy is evacuation, not staying to defend property — know your evacuation route

For large-quantity needs that exceed county program limits, [professional sandbag delivery](/sandbag-delivery/) and [sandbag filling services](/sandbag-filling-service/) are available throughout California.
