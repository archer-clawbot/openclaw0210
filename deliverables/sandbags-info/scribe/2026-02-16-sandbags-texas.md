## H1: Sandbags in Texas: City Programs, Harvey Lessons, and How to Prepare

Texas is the most flood-prone state in the United States by total flood events and deaths. The National Weather Service records more flood fatalities in Texas than in any other state in most years — a result of the state's enormous geographic diversity: Gulf Coast hurricane exposure, the notorious flash-flood corridor of the Hill Country, and the flat, poorly-draining prairies of North Texas.

Sandbag programs exist across the state, but they vary significantly by city and county. Knowing your local resources — and understanding how they've performed under pressure — is essential for any Texas homeowner in a flood-risk area.

---

## H2: Texas Flood Risk — What Makes It Different

### H3: The Flash Flood Corridor

Central Texas from San Antonio to Austin to Waco sits in what meteorologists call "Flash Flood Alley" — an area where intense rainfall events combine with limestone terrain that doesn't absorb water, producing rapid, often life-threatening flash flooding with little warning. This type of flooding often gives residents 30–90 minutes of warning, not 24–48 hours. That's why pre-staged sandbag supplies matter more in Texas than almost anywhere else.

### H3: Gulf Coast Tropical Systems

From Brownsville to Beaumont, the Texas Gulf Coast faces the same hurricane and tropical storm risk as Florida and Louisiana. Unlike Florida, much of the Texas Gulf Coast outside Houston is sparsely serviced by municipal flood programs. Rural coastal property owners often have no county distribution option and must rely entirely on pre-staged personal supplies.

### H3: North Texas Urban Flooding

The Dallas-Fort Worth Metroplex experiences significant urban flooding from intense convective storms — the flat terrain and large paved surface areas generate enormous runoff that overwhelms even modern storm drainage. Trinity River flooding affects portions of Dallas regularly.

## H2: The Harvey Context — What Texas Learned

Hurricane Harvey made landfall near Rockport in August 2017 as a Category 4 hurricane, then stalled over Southeast Texas and dumped an historic 60 inches of rain on portions of the Houston area — the highest rainfall total ever recorded from a U.S. tropical system.

### H3: What Worked

- Residents with pre-staged sandbags in flood-prone neighborhoods protected homes that had flooded in previous events
- Houston's distribution program, despite being overwhelmed, provided meaningful protection to tens of thousands of properties
- Reusable flood barriers (water tube systems) at commercial properties significantly outperformed sandbag systems in effectiveness and deployment speed

### H3: What Failed

- Distribution sites ran out of supplies within hours of Harvey's landfall forecast
- The volume of water Harvey produced overwhelmed any sandbag deployment in the most severely affected areas — 4-foot walls don't stop 6 feet of floodwater
- Many residents had not pre-staged any materials and were unable to access distribution sites once flooding began

### H3: The Harvey Lesson for Individual Homeowners

Pre-stage your materials before the season, not when a storm is forecast. Harvey's distribution failure was not a program failure — it was a capacity failure when demand vastly exceeded any reasonable planning assumption. Your county program is a supplement; your own pre-staged supply is your primary defense.

## H2: Houston Sandbag Program

Houston is the largest city in the most flood-prone state in the country. The city operates an extensive sandbag program through the Houston Public Works Department.

### H3: How to Access Sandbags in Houston

**Houston Public Works Sandbag Program:**
- **Hotline:** 311 (Houston 311 Service Line) — activated sandbag distribution sites are announced here
- **Normal operation:** Houston maintains sandbag distribution at its Public Works service centers throughout the year, not just during declared emergencies
- **Hurricane activation:** During tropical weather events, Houston activates additional sites at community centers and parks; announcements go through Houston Office of Emergency Management (HoustonEmergency.gov)
- **Standard limit:** 25 bags per household per day — among the most generous limits of any major Texas city

### H3: Harris County Programs

Unincorporated Harris County residents (outside Houston city limits) access programs through Harris County Flood Control District (HCFCD):
- **HCFCD Hotline:** 713-684-4000
- Harris County has historically maintained emergency sandbag distribution through Fire Marshal facilities and Public Works locations
- Harris County also operates the Harris County Office of Homeland Security & Emergency Management (HCOHEM), which coordinates with Houston OEM during major events

### H3: Houston-Area Private Resources

Given Houston's flood frequency, private sandbag supply is robust in the area. Hardware stores, landscape supply yards, and dedicated flood supply companies serve the market year-round. [Sandbag delivery services](/sandbag-delivery/) operate in the Houston market and can supplement county limits.

## H2: Dallas-Fort Worth Sandbag Programs

### H3: Dallas

The City of Dallas manages sandbag distribution through Dallas Water Utilities and the Office of Emergency Management:

- **311 Service:** Dallas 311 activates sandbag distribution information when significant flooding is forecast
- **Permanent year-round sites:** Dallas maintains some year-round sandbag access at Water Utilities facilities; call 311 for current locations
- **Trinity River awareness:** Properties in low-lying areas near Elm Fork and West Fork Trinity River corridors should maintain personal stockpiles given the frequency of minor overflow events

### H3: Fort Worth

Fort Worth Stormwater Management coordinates sandbag distribution:
- **Fort Worth 311** is the primary contact for sandbag site information
- Fort Worth activates distribution through its Parks & Recreation facilities during flood events
- The City of Fort Worth serves the broader Tarrant County area for most western DFW municipalities

### H3: Surrounding DFW Municipalities

Many smaller DFW cities (Frisco, McKinney, Plano, Arlington, Irving) operate their own independent sandbag programs. Contact your specific city's public works department — most have 311 systems or emergency management pages.

## H2: San Antonio Sandbag Program

San Antonio sits at the heart of Flash Flood Alley. The San Antonio River and its tributaries flood regularly, and the limestone terrain north of the city produces rapid-onset flash flooding.

### H3: City of San Antonio / Bexar County

San Antonio manages flood preparation through the City's Stormwater Management Department and Bexar County Emergency Management:

- **SA311:** The city's 311 line is the primary portal for sandbag distribution information
- **Stormwater Management:** 210-207-7023 — provides current sandbag site information year-round
- **Year-round access:** San Antonio typically maintains year-round sandbag distribution at stormwater maintenance yards; call 311 for locations
- **Flash flood emphasis:** Given the flash flood context, San Antonio Emergency Management emphasizes pre-staging personal supplies; 48-hour warning times cannot be assumed

### H3: Medina and Kendall County

Residents of the Hill Country counties surrounding San Antonio (Medina, Kendall, Comal, Guadalupe) often have limited municipal sandbag programs. In these areas, self-sufficiency is essential. Pre-stage at least 200–400 unfilled bags per property and maintain a sand supply on-site.

## H2: TxDOT's Role in Flood Preparedness

The Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT) plays a significant but often overlooked role in Texas flood response:

- TxDOT maintains highway right-of-way drainage that affects neighborhood flooding patterns
- TxDOT distributes sandbags to protect road infrastructure during flood events
- In some rural areas, TxDOT facilities serve as de facto sandbag distribution points during declared disasters

If your road access is threatened by flooding and your area lacks other resources, contacting your regional TxDOT district office (there are 25 regional districts) may connect you with materials.

**TxDOT Emergency Line:** 800-558-9368 (for road conditions and closures; some districts have separate flood resource lines)

## H2: Statewide Texas Resources

**Texas Division of Emergency Management (TDEM):** TDEM.Texas.gov — coordinates statewide disaster response including flood events. During presidentially declared disasters, TDEM coordinates with FEMA on individual assistance and public assistance programs.

**Texas 211:** During disaster declarations, 211 is activated statewide to connect residents with local resources including sandbag distribution.

**Texas Flood Registry:** TexasFloodRegistry.org — tracks flood events and provides data on Texas flood risk by location.

**Know your FEMA flood zone:** Check MSC.FEMA.gov for your property's flood zone. Properties in FEMA Special Flood Hazard Areas in Texas face elevated risk and should maintain robust personal stockpiles.

For large-quantity needs beyond county program limits, [professional sandbag delivery](/sandbag-delivery/) and [filling services](/sandbag-filling-service/) operate across Texas and can supply properties without waiting for county distribution activation.
