# Sandbags in Mississippi: Flood Prep and Distribution Guide

Mississippi faces some of the most persistent and varied flood risks in the country. From the Mississippi River corridor in the west to the Gulf Coast in the south and the inland river systems that cross every region of the state, flooding is not an occasional disruption — it's a predictable, recurring challenge for hundreds of thousands of residents and property owners.

This guide covers where to get sandbags in Mississippi, how to work with state and county emergency management resources, and what Mississippi homeowners should know about flood preparation specific to their region.

---

## Mississippi's Flood Risk: What You're Dealing With

### The Mississippi River

The western edge of the state sits along one of the most flood-prone river corridors in North America. The Mississippi River regularly crests into surrounding lowlands, particularly in the Delta region. Flooding along the Mississippi River corridor affects communities from Tunica in the north to Natchez in the south, with agricultural and residential areas in the Delta facing the highest sustained risk.

River flooding here isn't always sudden. Major Mississippi River flood events can develop over weeks as upstream precipitation raises water levels incrementally. This typically provides meaningful warning time — but the duration of flooding can stretch for months in extreme years.

### Gulf Coast and Hurricane Season

The Mississippi Gulf Coast — including Gulfport, Biloxi, Ocean Springs, and Pass Christian — faces a distinct and more intense flood threat: hurricane storm surge. Gulf Coast counties are among the most hurricane-vulnerable in the nation. Storm surge from a major Gulf hurricane can push seawater 15–20 miles inland, overwhelming conventional flood protection.

Sandbags play a supporting role in hurricane preparation, but their effectiveness is limited against the surge from a direct major hurricane impact. The primary value of sandbags along the Gulf Coast is in protecting against the outer storm bands and minor surge events associated with tropical storms and the weaker edges of larger hurricanes.

### Inland Flooding

Throughout central and northern Mississippi, rivers including the Pearl, Yazoo, Big Black, Tombigbee, and Pascagoula regularly experience flood events tied to heavy seasonal rainfall. Pearl River flooding is a recurring issue for the Jackson metro area. Hattiesburg and Laurel have experienced significant flooding from the Leaf River and its tributaries.

Flash flooding is also a significant risk statewide. Mississippi receives an average of 55–60 inches of rainfall annually, and intense summer thunderstorm cells can produce 3–5 inches in a matter of hours, overwhelming urban and suburban drainage systems.

## Where to Get Sandbags in Mississippi

### MEMA — Mississippi Emergency Management Agency

The Mississippi Emergency Management Agency (MEMA) coordinates the state's response to flood events, including the logistics of sandbag distribution. During declared emergencies or significant flood events, MEMA activates the State Emergency Operations Center and coordinates with county emergency management offices to establish distribution points.

**Contact MEMA:**
- Website: [ms.gov/mema](https://www.ms.gov/mema)
- Main line: (601) 933-6362
- During active emergencies, check MEMA's social media channels and website for distribution site updates

MEMA does not typically maintain direct-to-resident distribution sites statewide — that function is handled at the county level. MEMA coordinates logistics, supplies, and resource allocation to support county operations.

### County Emergency Management Offices

Your first call for sandbags in Mississippi should be your county's Emergency Management office. County programs vary significantly — some maintain year-round sandbag distribution, others activate only during events.

**Key contacts for high-risk areas:**

- **Hinds County** (Jackson): Hinds County Emergency Management — (601) 857-5376
- **Harrison County** (Gulfport, Biloxi area): Harrison County Emergency Management — check harrisonems.com
- **Forrest County** (Hattiesburg): Forrest County Emergency Management — (601) 545-6050
- **Jackson County** (Pascagoula area): Jackson County Emergency Management — (228) 769-3111
- **DeSoto County** (Southaven/Memphis metro): DeSoto County Emergency Management — (662) 469-8000

During active flood events, call 211 for referrals to the nearest sandbag distribution point.

### Hardware Stores and Home Improvement Centers

Outside of emergency distribution events, sandbags are available at hardware stores, farm supply retailers, and building supply centers across Mississippi. Major chains including Home Depot, Lowe's, and Tractor Supply typically stock sandbags year-round. Coastal and Delta locations often stock higher quantities during hurricane season (June–November).

## Flood Preparation Tips for Mississippi Residents

### Start Before the Season

Mississippi's peak flood risk periods align with two distinct seasons:
- **Spring (February–April)**: River flooding from winter and spring precipitation upstream and locally
- **Hurricane season (June–November)**: Gulf Coast storm surge and inland flooding from tropical systems

Begin your flood preparation before these windows open. Waiting until a watch or warning is issued means competing with thousands of other residents for sandbags, sand, and supplies.

### Know Your Flood Zone

FEMA maintains Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) for every county in Mississippi. Check your property's flood zone designation at [msc.fema.gov](https://msc.fema.gov). Properties in Zone AE or VE face the highest risk and should have a documented flood response plan.

### Prepare Your Sandbag Supply

For properties with active flood risk, keeping 50–100 sandbags and a supply of sand on hand before storm season provides a critical advantage. When a system is 24–48 hours out, distribution sites may run out. Having your own supply means you're not dependent on municipal availability.

### Protect Key Openings First

In most residential flooding scenarios, water enters through the lowest points: garage doors, sliding glass doors, crawl space vents, and exterior basement access. Prioritize these openings in your sandbag deployment plan.

For detailed technique guidance, see [how to stack sandbags](/how-to-stack-sandbags/).

### Register with Your County

Many Mississippi counties maintain voluntary registries for residents who may need assistance during evacuations or flood response — particularly elderly residents, people with disabilities, and households without transportation. Contact your county emergency management office to ask about these programs.

## Key Flood-Prone Areas in Mississippi

### Jackson Metro Area

Jackson and the surrounding communities in Hinds and Rankin counties are primarily at risk from Pearl River flooding. The Pearl has experienced major flood events including significant 2020 flooding that inundated parts of the city. Low-lying neighborhoods near the river and Ross Barnett Reservoir spillway areas require the most preparation attention.

### Gulfport and Biloxi

Harrison County's coastal communities face recurring storm surge risk. Hurricane Katrina's 2005 impact remains the benchmark event for Gulf Coast flood planning in Mississippi, but even lesser tropical systems regularly threaten the immediate coast. Both cities maintain active emergency management programs with established sandbag distribution protocols.

### Hattiesburg

The Hub City and surrounding Forrest and Lamar counties are in the path of Leaf River flooding. Hattiesburg has experienced repeated significant flood events, including major 2013 and 2020 flooding. The urban core and lower-lying residential areas near the river system are most vulnerable.

### The Delta

Communities throughout the Mississippi Delta — including Greenville, Vicksburg, Yazoo City, and the agricultural communities of Bolivar, Washington, and Sunflower counties — face the most sustained river flooding risk in the state. Emergency management programs here are generally well-developed given the frequency of events.
